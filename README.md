# QuantPricer — Multi-Model Derivatives Pricing Engine

A comprehensive C++17 quantitative finance library that implements the same pricing models, risk engines, and market infrastructure used by quantitative teams at investment banks and hedge funds. Covers derivatives pricing (Black-Scholes, Heston, Merton), numerical methods (Monte Carlo, finite differences, binomial trees), risk management (VaR, CVaR, stress testing), fixed income analytics (yield curves, rate models), and exchange-grade order book mechanics.

Built from scratch with zero external dependencies beyond the C++ Standard Library — every algorithm is implemented by hand to demonstrate deep understanding of the mathematics and systems design behind modern quantitative finance.

**17 header modules | 142 tests | 11 showcase plots | 15 example programs | Interactive Streamlit dashboard**

```
make release     # build everything (-O3 optimized)
make run-tests   # 142 tests, 0 failures
make showcase    # generate all plots below
make dashboard   # interactive dashboard on localhost:8501
```

## What This Project Does

This engine solves the central problem of quantitative finance: **given a financial contract with uncertain future payoffs, what is its fair price today, and how does that price change when market conditions move?**

Every bank, hedge fund, and trading firm needs answers to these questions in real time. The models here are the industry-standard tools for answering them:

- **Options pricing** — Computing the theoretical value of derivatives contracts (calls, puts, exotics) so traders know whether the market price is cheap or expensive
- **Greeks/risk sensitivities** — Measuring how a portfolio's value changes with spot price (Delta), volatility (Vega), time (Theta), and rates (Rho), so risk managers can hedge exposure
- **Volatility modeling** — Capturing the market's implied volatility surface to price exotic options consistently with liquid vanilla markets
- **Monte Carlo simulation** — Pricing path-dependent and multi-asset options where no closed-form solution exists
- **Yield curve construction** — Building the discount curve from market instruments to value any future cash flow
- **Risk measurement** — Quantifying portfolio losses under extreme scenarios (VaR, CVaR) as required by Basel III/IV regulations
- **Order book simulation** — Modeling the exchange matching engine to understand market microstructure, queue priority, and execution quality

## Volatility Surface

**What it shows:** Three 3D implied volatility surfaces generated by the Heston stochastic volatility model, each with a different spot-vol correlation (rho).

![Vol Surface](showcase_plots/01_vol_surface_3rho.png)

- **rho = -0.9**: steep negative skew (crash protection is expensive — out-of-the-money puts cost more in vol terms)
- **rho = -0.5**: moderate skew (most realistic for equity markets, where large drops coincide with vol spikes)
- **rho = 0.0**: symmetric smile (no leverage effect — typical of FX or commodity markets)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Every options desk at Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan, or Citadel maintains a live volatility surface. It is the single most important input for pricing exotic derivatives. If your surface is wrong, every exotic you price off it is mispriced. The vol surface encodes the market's view on the probability distribution of future returns — it tells you that the market assigns higher probability to large downside moves than a lognormal model would predict (the "skew"), and that short-dated options exhibit higher curvature than long-dated ones (the "term structure"). Traders who understand vol surface dynamics can identify mispricings in structured products, design hedges that account for skew risk, and build trading strategies around vol regime changes.

![Vol Smile](showcase_plots/02_vol_smile.png)

The smile cross-section shows implied vol vs strike at a fixed maturity — this is what traders look at when quoting options and is the basis for the SABR and SVI parameterizations used on production desks.

## Greeks Dashboard

**What it shows:** All five first-order sensitivities (Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta, Rho) of a European call option as a function of the underlying spot price, computed analytically from Black-Scholes.

![Greeks](showcase_plots/03_greeks_dashboard.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Greeks are the language of risk management. Every trading desk in the world monitors Greeks in real time:

- **Delta** tells you how many shares to hold to hedge your option position. A market maker who sells 1000 calls with Delta = 0.5 must buy 500 shares to be delta-neutral. This is what "delta hedging" means — it's done continuously, thousands of times per day across every options desk globally.
- **Gamma** measures how fast your Delta changes. High Gamma near expiry means your hedge is unstable and you need to rebalance frequently, incurring transaction costs. This is the fundamental tension in options market making.
- **Vega** is your exposure to volatility changes. If you're long Vega and the VIX spikes 5 points, you profit. Vol trading desks at firms like Susquehanna and Optiver manage Vega exposure as their primary risk dimension.
- **Theta** is the cost of holding an option — the "time decay" that options sellers collect. Short-dated options lose value fastest, which is why selling weekly options is a popular income strategy.
- **Rho** measures interest rate sensitivity. Less important for short-dated equity options, but critical for long-dated FX options and the entire fixed income derivatives book.

Banks compute Greeks on millions of positions every night in their risk runs. Regulatory capital (Basel III) is partially determined by aggregate Greeks. Getting Greeks wrong can lead to catastrophic hedging failures — the 2012 "London Whale" loss at JP Morgan was partly a Greeks computation issue.

## Monte Carlo Convergence

**What it shows:** Price convergence and standard error reduction as the number of simulation paths increases, at three different volatility levels (sigma = 15%, 25%, 40%).

![MC Convergence](showcase_plots/04_mc_convergence.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Monte Carlo is the workhorse method for pricing complex derivatives that have no closed-form solution — Asian options, barrier options, multi-asset baskets, autocallables, and virtually every structured product sold by investment banks. The convergence plot demonstrates the fundamental trade-off: more paths = more accuracy, but the standard error decreases as O(1/sqrt(N)), meaning you need 4x the paths to halve the error. This is why variance reduction techniques (antithetic variates, control variates, importance sampling — all implemented in this library) are essential in production. A desk pricing 10,000 exotic options per night needs each price computed in seconds, not minutes. The plot also shows that higher volatility requires more paths for the same accuracy — a practical calibration insight for production MC engines.

## Model Comparison — BS vs Heston vs Merton

**What it shows:** Implied volatility smiles produced by three different models: flat Black-Scholes, Heston stochastic volatility (rho = -0.7), and Merton jump-diffusion.

![Model Comparison](showcase_plots/05_model_comparison.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Real markets exhibit volatility smiles and skews that Black-Scholes cannot produce. This plot shows exactly *how* different models depart from BS:

- **Heston** (stochastic vol, rho = -0.7) produces a smooth negative skew because spot and vol are negatively correlated — when stocks drop, vol rises. This is the "leverage effect" observed across all equity markets since at least the 1987 crash. Heston is the standard model for equity vol desks.
- **Merton** (jump-diffusion) produces fatter tails because it adds discrete Poisson jumps to the diffusion. The implied vol smile is more pronounced in short maturities where jump risk dominates. This model is used for pricing options on assets with event risk — earnings announcements, FDA decisions, geopolitical events.

Choosing the right model matters enormously. A bank that prices a 1-year autocallable note using flat Black-Scholes vol instead of a skew-aware model like Heston will systematically misprice the embedded barrier and coupon features, potentially losing millions over a large issuance program. The model comparison framework here lets you quantify exactly how much each modeling choice matters.

## Barrier Options

**What it shows:** Down-and-out and down-and-in call prices at three volatility levels (sigma = 15%, 25%, 35%).

![Barrier](showcase_plots/06_barrier_analysis.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Barrier options are among the most actively traded exotic derivatives, particularly in FX and structured products. They are cheaper than vanilla options because the barrier feature limits the payoff in certain scenarios:

- A **knock-out** option dies worthless if the underlying hits the barrier — it provides the same upside as a vanilla call but at a discount, in exchange for the risk of early termination. Higher volatility makes knock-out options *cheaper* because the barrier is more likely to be hit.
- A **knock-in** option only activates when the barrier is hit — it's insurance against a specific market scenario.
- The sum of knock-in + knock-out always equals the vanilla price (in-out parity) — this is a critical arbitrage relationship that desks use to cross-check their pricing.

The BGK continuity correction implemented here is essential for practical barrier pricing: real barriers are monitored daily (discrete), but analytic formulas assume continuous monitoring. The correction adjusts for this gap and prevents systematic mispricing that has historically cost banks real money.

## Correlation Trade — Multi-Asset Options

**What it shows:** How correlation between assets affects the prices of basket, best-of, and worst-of options, computed via Cholesky-decomposed Monte Carlo.

![Correlation](showcase_plots/07_correlation_trade.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Correlation trading is one of the most profitable and intellectually demanding areas in structured products. This plot captures the key insight that drives billions of dollars in structured note issuance every year:

- **Best-of call** price *decreases* with correlation — when assets move together, the "best" performer doesn't stand out as much. Low correlation = more dispersion = higher best-of value.
- **Worst-of call** price *increases* with correlation — when assets move together, the "worst" performer isn't as bad. Worst-of options are extremely popular in structured products (autocallables, reverse convertibles) precisely because they are cheap when correlation is moderate.
- **Basket call** price *increases* with correlation because basket volatility = sigma * sqrt(rho) for equal-weight portfolios. This is the diversification effect — the fundamental principle behind portfolio theory.

Banks sell billions of dollars of worst-of autocallable notes every year. The correlation exposure embedded in these products is one of the largest risk concentrations on bank balance sheets. The 2008 financial crisis caused massive losses on correlation books when realized correlations spiked to near 1.0, collapsing the value of dispersion trades. Understanding this plot is understanding one of the most important dynamics in modern structured finance.

## American Options — Early Exercise Premium

**What it shows:** American put price vs European put price at three interest rate levels (r = 2%, 5%, 10%).

![American](showcase_plots/08_american_options.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** All listed equity options in the US are American-style (early exercise allowed). The early exercise premium — the extra value of an American option over its European counterpart — is not a theoretical curiosity; it's real money:

- For deep in-the-money puts, early exercise lets you receive cash (the strike price) sooner and earn interest on it. Higher interest rates make early exercise more attractive, which is why the premium widens with r.
- Market makers must model the early exercise boundary correctly, or they will be systematically exercised against by informed counterparties at disadvantageous times.
- The Longstaff-Schwartz algorithm implemented here (least-squares Monte Carlo) is the industry standard for American option pricing in multi-factor models where tree methods become impractical.

The CRR binomial tree used here converges to the correct American price and simultaneously gives you the optimal exercise boundary — critical for understanding when rational early exercise occurs.

## Finite Difference Method

**What it shows:** Crank-Nicolson PDE solution on a 400x2000 grid overlaid on Black-Scholes analytic, with error analysis.

![FDM](showcase_plots/09_fdm_solution.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Finite difference methods solve the pricing PDE directly on a grid, making them the method of choice when:

- You need to price American options with continuous dividends (tree methods struggle with continuous yields)
- You're working with local volatility models (Dupire) where the vol is a function of spot and time
- You need to price barrier options with complex barrier schedules
- You need Greeks that are smooth and stable (FDM Greeks come "for free" from the grid)

The Crank-Nicolson scheme used here is second-order accurate in both time and space, and unconditionally stable — this is why it's the default PDE solver in production systems like Numerix, FINCAD, and most bank internal libraries. The Thomas algorithm (implemented in the matrix module) solves the resulting tridiagonal systems in O(N) time, making the method extremely efficient. Error < 0.001 against analytic Black-Scholes validates the implementation.

## Yield Curves & Rate Models

**What it shows:** Nelson-Siegel yield curve shapes (normal, flat, inverted) and Vasicek vs CIR model-implied yield curves.

![Yield Curves](showcase_plots/10_yield_curves.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** The yield curve is the foundation of all fixed income pricing. Every bond, swap, mortgage, and structured note is valued by discounting cash flows using the yield curve:

- A **normal curve** (short rates < long rates) is the typical shape, reflecting the term premium for lending money over longer horizons. It signals economic expansion.
- A **flat curve** often appears during monetary policy transitions and signals uncertainty about the economic outlook.
- An **inverted curve** (short rates > long rates) has preceded every US recession since 1970. When the 2-year Treasury yield exceeds the 10-year yield, it signals that the market expects the Fed to cut rates in response to a coming downturn.

The **Nelson-Siegel** parameterization (3 parameters controlling level, slope, and curvature) is used by central banks worldwide — the ECB publishes daily Nelson-Siegel parameters for the euro area yield curve. The **Vasicek** and **CIR** short-rate models generate yield curves dynamically and are used to price interest rate derivatives (caps, floors, swaptions). The CIR model's square-root diffusion ensures rates stay positive (satisfying the Feller condition) — a crucial property that Vasicek lacks.

## Payoff Profiles

**What it shows:** Classic call, put, and straddle payoffs at expiry (dotted) vs present value with time value (solid).

![Payoffs](showcase_plots/11_payoff_profiles.png)

**Why it matters in real finance:** Understanding payoff profiles is the first step in options literacy. The gap between the expiry payoff (hockey stick) and the present value (smooth curve) is the **time value** — the premium you pay for optionality. This time value is what the entire derivatives pricing theory (Black-Scholes, stochastic calculus, risk-neutral pricing) is designed to compute. The straddle (long call + long put at the same strike) is a pure volatility bet — you profit if the stock moves significantly in either direction, and lose if it stays near the strike. Straddles are routinely traded around earnings announcements and Fed meetings when traders expect large moves but are uncertain about direction.

## Architecture

```
include/
  payoff/         PayOff hierarchy (call, put, digital, power)
  option/         VanillaOption, HestonParams, MertonJumpParams
  greeks/         BS analytics + FD + MC Greeks engine
  mc/             European, Asian, Heston, Merton MC + Longstaff-Schwartz
  fdm/            Crank-Nicolson FDM solver
  tree/           CRR binomial tree (European + American)
  vol/            Implied vol (Newton-Raphson, bisection) + vol surface
  barrier/        8 barrier types (Haug analytic) + MC + BGK correction
  multi_asset/    Basket, best-of, worst-of, Margrabe + Cholesky MC
  risk/           VaR, CVaR, Sharpe/Sortino/Calmar, stress testing
  fixed_income/   Yield curves, bootstrapping, Nelson-Siegel, bonds, duration/DV01
  rates/          Vasicek, CIR, Hull-White + bond options
  orderbook/      CLOB matching engine (price-time priority)
  matrix/         QMatrix, LU, Thomas, Cholesky decomposition
  rng/            Mersenne Twister, Box-Muller, correlated normals
```

All modules are header-only with a single `src/quantpricer.cpp` compilation unit. Zero external dependencies — everything from the Mersenne Twister RNG to the Cholesky decomposition is implemented from first principles using only the C++ Standard Library.
